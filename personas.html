Part 2

Strategic Insights

What the persona research tells us about how to position the agent builder page — framing, structure, and what to avoid.

Framing

Don't lead with "build your own agent"

For CIO, COO, VP of Ops — that sounds like a developer tool. They don't want to build; they want outcomes. Lead with a specific agent that solves a specific problem. Show the builder as customization, not construction. "Build" is the last step, not the headline.

Real pain

The ICP's #1 pain is operational overhead — not "needing AI"

Every persona (IT, PMO, Marketing) describes the same underlying problem differently: too much coordination work, not enough time for strategy. The page should speak to that directly — not AI features, not technology. The agent is a solution to a workflow problem they already feel.

Competitive positioning

Competitors frame agents as assistants. monday.com's differentiator is agents that work on your behalf.

Asana, Notion, ClickUp all position AI as something that helps you work faster — co-pilot framing. monday's differentiator is agents that do the work autonomously: on a schedule, triggered by events, even when you're not there. That's a fundamentally different promise. It should be front and center.

Gap

RevOps is underserved in the persona briefs — but it's in the target ICP

None of the three persona briefs (IT, PMO, Marketing) covers RevOps or Sales Ops — but Head of RevOps and Operations Manager are on the hackathon ICP list. Deal tracking, pipeline risk, forecast reporting are perfect agent builder territory and highly tangible. Worth addressing on the page.

Trust & Control

Decision-makers need to see governance before they'll say yes

CIOs, CTOs, and IT Directors are gatekeepers — they evaluate tools for the org, not just for themselves. Trust and control signals (per-board permissions, human-in-the-loop, audit log, admin governance, cost center) aren't nice-to-haves on this page — they're table stakes for this audience. If they don't see them, the page won't convert.

Part 3

Recommended Page Structure

A page flow that follows the persona logic — from pain to proof to permission.

1

Open with the pain

Your team is drowning in coordination. Every channel, every update, every status report — all manual. This is what the decision-maker feels every day. Name it before you pitch anything.

2

Show 3–4 concrete agents

Each with: who it's for + what triggers it + what it does. Short. Specific. Real. Not feature lists — before/after moments. Lead with the most relatable (post-meeting tasks or weekly report).

3

Reveal: pre-built and customizable in minutes

"These are ready to go. Modify them for your workflow in minutes — or build from scratch." This is where the builder enters. Not as the lead, but as the unlock.

4

Address trust & control

Per-board permissions. Human-in-the-loop. Audit log. Admin governance. Cost center. This is where the CIO/CTO moves from interested to approved. Don't bury it — it earns the deal.

5

Close with the promise

"Your team leads. Agents handle the rest." Short, clear, human. Ends on empowerment, not technology.

⚠️ Note on naming

"Agent Builder" as a product name may create friction with this ICP — it implies they need to build something. Consider whether the page leads with the outcome (what the agent does) and introduces the builder as a capability, not the feature name.
